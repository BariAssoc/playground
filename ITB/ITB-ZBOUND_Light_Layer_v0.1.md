## PART 1 — WHAT TIRZEPATIDE IS

Tirzepatide is a peptide medication that activates two hormone receptors at the same time: the GLP-1 receptor and the GIP receptor. These receptors live in the pancreas (where they help regulate insulin), the stomach (where they slow how fast food leaves the stomach), and in parts of the brain that decide how hungry you feel. Tirzepatide was engineered as an acylated peptide that activates GIP and GLP-1 receptors, which mediate insulin secretion and are also expressed in brain regions regulating food intake.¹

This dual mechanism is what makes tirzepatide different from earlier medications in the GLP-1 family. Semaglutide (Wegovy, Ozempic) acts on one receptor; tirzepatide acts on two. The practical result is stronger and more consistent weight reduction for most patients.

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Class | Dual GIP / GLP-1 receptor agonist |
| Brand names | Zepbound® (obesity), Mounjaro® (type 2 diabetes) |
| Route | Subcutaneous injection (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm) |
| Frequency | Once weekly, any time of day, with or without meals |
| Elimination half-life | Approximately 5 days |
| Time to steady state | Approximately 4 weeks on weekly dosing |
| FDA approval — obesity | November 2023 |

Population pharmacokinetic analyses show tirzepatide has a half-life of approximately 5 days, which supports once-weekly subcutaneous dosing with sustained exposure.² The 5-day half-life is the reason the medication is dosed once a week rather than daily — and the reason why missing a single dose does not wipe out your treatment. The medication is still in your system for many days after each injection.

## PART 2 — THE JOURNEY YOU ARE ABOUT TO START

Tirzepatide is dose-escalated — meaning you start low and step up over time. This schedule is part of the FDA-approved label and is how your physician will prescribe it.³

| Month | Weekly dose | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2.5 mg | Introduction — body adjusts, usually no weight loss expected |
| 2 | 5.0 mg | First therapeutic dose |
| 3 | 7.5 mg | Step up |
| 4 | 10.0 mg | Maintenance option |
| 5 | 12.5 mg | Step up |
| 6+ | 15.0 mg | Maximum dose |

This schedule exists for one reason: to let your body adapt. Gradual dose escalation every 4 weeks minimizes gastrointestinal side effects — the most common reason patients stop treatment.² Skipping steps or starting high is not done because the stomach and gut need time to adjust.

Not every patient needs to reach 15 mg. Some patients respond well at 10 mg and stay there. Your physician will decide based on your weight trajectory and how you tolerate each step.

## PART 3 — WHAT THE EVIDENCE ACTUALLY SHOWS

The major trial for Zepbound in obesity is called **SURMOUNT-1**, published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in 2022. This is the study that led to FDA approval for weight management.

**What SURMOUNT-1 tested:** 2,539 adults with obesity or overweight with a weight-related comorbidity, but without diabetes, randomly assigned to tirzepatide (5, 10, or 15 mg) or placebo, for 72 weeks. In the 72-week trial, tirzepatide 5 mg, 10 mg, or 15 mg once weekly provided substantial and sustained reductions in body weight.⁴

**Average weight reduction over 72 weeks:**

| Dose | Weight reduction (average) |
|---|---|
| Placebo | ~3% |
| 5 mg | ~16% |
| 10 mg | ~21% |
| 15 mg | ~22.5% |

In SURMOUNT-1, about nine out of ten participants taking tirzepatide lost weight, and fat mass reduction was approximately three times greater than lean mass reduction (33.9% versus 10.9%).⁴

**The 3-year follow-up** published in *NEJM* in 2025 extended these findings further. At 176 weeks, only 1.3% of patients with obesity and prediabetes taking tirzepatide progressed to type 2 diabetes, compared with 13.3% of patients on placebo.⁵ This is a roughly 90% reduction in the chance of developing type 2 diabetes over three years.

**Maintenance — SURMOUNT-4:** A separate trial asked what happens if you stop the medication. Participants who completed 36 weeks of tirzepatide and then continued the drug had a −5.5% weight change through week 88, while those switched to placebo regained 14.0% — a difference of about 19 percentage points.⁶ In plain language: the medication works while you take it. Stopping it without a maintenance plan leads to regain.

This is important for Mark's case and anyone in his situation. The long-term question is not only "will the medication work?" — it does, reliably — but **"what is the plan for maintaining the result?"** This is where BariAccess enters.

## PART 4 — SAFETY: WHAT TO EXPECT AND WHEN TO CALL

Every medication has a safety profile. You deserve to know yours honestly.

### The Boxed Warning

Zepbound carries an FDA boxed warning — the strongest warning level. In rats, tirzepatide causes dose-dependent and treatment-duration-dependent thyroid C-cell tumors at clinically relevant exposures; human relevance has not been determined, and Zepbound is contraindicated in patients with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2).³

**What this means for Mark:** You have reported no personal or family history of thyroid cancer or MEN 2. Your physician has confirmed this is not a contraindication for you. The boxed warning is documented for informed consent purposes.

### Common Side Effects (Mostly Gastrointestinal)

A systematic review of six randomized controlled trials (4,586 patients) found nausea in 20.43% of tirzepatide-treated patients (versus 10.47% of comparators) and vomiting in 9.05% (versus 4.86%).⁷ These are almost always during the dose-escalation phase and usually resolve as your body adapts.

**What you may experience, especially in months 1–3:**

- Nausea, usually mild to moderate
- Decreased appetite (this is part of how the medication works)
- Diarrhea or constipation
- Fatigue
- Belching or reflux
- Injection site reactions

**Practical management:**

- Smaller, more frequent meals
- Avoid greasy, fried, or very rich foods in the first days after a dose
- Stay hydrated (this is important enough that Part 6 is dedicated to it)
- Eat slowly — your stomach empties slower on this medication

### When to Call Your Physician Immediately

| Symptom | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Severe, persistent abdominal pain | Possible pancreatitis |
| Pain in right upper abdomen, fever, yellowing of skin | Possible gallbladder problem |
| Severe vomiting or diarrhea that doesn't stop | Dehydration risk |
| Very dark urine or no urine for many hours | Acute kidney injury risk |
| Trouble breathing, swelling of face or throat | Allergic reaction |
| Persistent hoarseness, neck lump, trouble swallowing | Thyroid evaluation needed |

Tirzepatide is associated with gastrointestinal adverse reactions that can lead to dehydration and, in severe cases, acute kidney injury.⁸ This is why monitoring — which BariAccess will do — matters.

## PART 5 — THE PROTEIN STORY (CRITICAL FOR MARK)

Here is where your age becomes relevant, Mark. You are 63. You are about to lose a substantial amount of weight. **How you lose that weight matters at least as much as how much you lose.**

### The Muscle Loss Risk

When anyone loses weight, some of that weight comes from fat and some from lean body mass (which includes muscle). This is true on any diet, on any medication, with any approach. The question is: how much?

GLP-1 receptor agonist therapy can produce total weight loss of up to 25%, but studies also show significant loss of lean body mass — reaching 15% to 40% of total weight lost.⁹ For older adults, this is the central concern. Losing muscle at 63 is not the same as losing muscle at 33. Muscle takes longer to rebuild, and muscle loss contributes to sarcopenia, falls, and loss of independence.

In the SURMOUNT-1 DXA subset, tirzepatide-treated participants lost approximately 11% of lean soft tissue compared with 34% of fat mass — about 26% of weight loss came from lean tissue.¹⁰

**For Mark specifically:** Losing 81 lb means losing roughly 20 lb of lean tissue if the average SURMOUNT ratio holds. That is unacceptable for a 63-year-old without a muscle-preservation strategy in place.

### The Protein Answer

The evidence is consistent and actionable.

The PROT-AGE international study group recommends 1.0–1.5 g of protein per kg of body weight per day for individuals older than 65, and the Nordic Nutrition Recommendations suggest 1.2–1.4 g/kg/day for healthy older adults.¹¹

A moderately high-protein diet of 1.2 g/kg body weight per day is more effective than the standard 0.8 g/kg recommendation in preserving muscle mass, enhancing strength, and improving body composition in older adults.¹²

**Mark's math:**

- Target weight ~154 lb (~70 kg)
- Protein target at 1.2–1.3 g/kg = **84 to 91 grams per day**
- Mark's reported intake: **90 g/day whey isolate** (Naked brand) = **1.29 g/kg — exactly in the target range**

This is why the BariAccess protocol built around 90 g whey isolate daily is not arbitrary. It is the dose the evidence supports, at the age the evidence specifically addresses, using the protein type the evidence prefers.

### Why Whey, Specifically

Whey protein stimulates postprandial muscle protein deposition in older men more efficiently than casein or casein hydrolysate, supporting the "fast" versus "slow" protein distinction.¹³

The leucine dose needed to maximally stimulate muscle protein synthesis in older persons is approximately 3–4 g per meal, which corresponds to roughly 25–30 g of protein per meal.¹⁴

Whey isolate is leucine-dense, which matters because older muscle is "anabolic resistant" — it takes more of the right stimulus to trigger protein synthesis than younger muscle does. Whey delivers that stimulus faster and more efficiently than most other protein sources.

### The Gold-Standard Evidence

The single most relevant study to Mark's situation was published in the *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* in 2015. In a 13-week randomized controlled trial of obese older adults (mean age 63, mean BMI 33), a high whey protein, leucine, and vitamin D-enriched supplement preserved appendicular muscle mass during intentional weight loss while the control group lost muscle.¹⁵

Mean age 63. Mean BMI 33. Intentional weight loss. Whey, leucine, and vitamin D.

This is Mark's demographic almost exactly.

## PART 6 — THE HYDRATION STORY

Tirzepatide slows how fast food leaves your stomach. This is part of what makes you feel full longer and eat less. It also means fluid intake can drop without you noticing — because you are not as thirsty either.

**The risk chain:**

```
Slower gastric emptying + reduced appetite
  ↓
Lower fluid intake
  ↓
GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea) if present
  ↓
Dehydration
  ↓
Acute kidney injury (rare but documented)
```

**Daily target (general guidance, confirm with your physician):** Roughly 2.5 to 3 liters of total fluid intake per day during active dose-escalation and weight loss phases; more on injection day, more in hot weather or after exercise.

**BariAccess hydration tracking** will flag:

- Total fluid intake falling below a rolling daily baseline
- Any day with more than two GI symptoms reported
- Urine output changes (via patient self-report)
- Resting heart rate elevation combined with low hydration (wearable data)

## PART 7 — THE HbA1c STORY (AND WHY YOURS MATTERS)

HbA1c is a blood test that reflects your average blood sugar over the past 2 to 3 months. The standard American Diabetes Association thresholds:

| HbA1c | Category |
|---|---|
| Below 5.7% | Normoglycemia (normal) |
| 5.7% to 6.4% | Prediabetes |
| 6.5% or higher | Diabetes |

**Mark's baseline is assumed to have been in the 6.0–6.3% range** — consistent with his BMI of 35.7 at intake. At year 2, your HbA1c is **5.8%**.

This is a meaningful improvement. You are now sitting at the low edge of prediabetes, essentially at the threshold of normal. Tirzepatide is doing exactly what the evidence predicts.

In the 3-year SURMOUNT-1 analysis, participants with obesity and prediabetes treated with tirzepatide achieved mean weight reduction of up to 22.9%, and 94.5% had reverted to normoglycemia at week 176.¹⁶

Over 72 weeks of treatment, tirzepatide at all doses produced significant reductions in HbA1c, fasting glucose, insulin, body weight, and HOMA2-IR, with improvements appearing within the first 12 weeks and consistent across glycemic subgroups.¹⁷

What this means for Mark: the medication is not only managing your weight — it is remapping your metabolic risk. The HbA1c drop is evidence that your insulin sensitivity is improving and your pancreas is working less hard. This is the kind of improvement that changes long-term outcomes for heart disease, kidney disease, and dementia risk — not just waist size.

## PART 8 — THE ADHERENCE REALITY (AND WHY BARIACCESS MATTERS)

Here is the honest picture of what happens to most patients on GLP-1 medications in the real world — outside of clinical trials:

In a large academic obesity clinic cohort, median persistence with GLP-1 receptor agonists was 10.7 months, with 50% of patients discontinuing therapy by 12 months.¹⁸

A retrospective cohort study of 125,474 US adults with overweight or obesity who newly initiated a dual-labeled GLP-1 receptor agonist followed discontinuation and reinitiation patterns over 2 years, documenting substantial drop-off in the first year of therapy.¹⁹

**Translation:** About half of patients stop within a year. Many restart, some do not. The medication works — but adherence is where real life breaks down.

**The reasons it breaks down:**

- GI side effects during escalation (preventable with the right support)
- Cost and insurance hurdles
- Lack of structured monitoring
- No plan for maintenance phase
- No system for catching early problems before they become reasons to quit
- Social context: travel, work, family changes, illness

**This is the gap BariAccess fills.** Not by prescribing — your external physician does that. By **wrapping your treatment in a monitoring, education, and adherence infrastructure** that catches problems at the pre-signal stage, before they become the reason you stop.

The evidence is clear about what works: continuous engagement, behavioral anchoring, biometric tracking, structured protein and hydration support, and a human in the loop (your Master Barista) who actually knows your case.

**Mark is a 4.5-year adherence outlier.** The median patient quits at 10.7 months. Mark is still engaged at year 4 and beyond. That outcome is not accidental. It is the product of exactly the kind of infrastructure BariAccess is built to deliver.
